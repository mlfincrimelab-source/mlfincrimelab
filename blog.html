MAS AML/CFT July 2025 Updates: What Every Singapore Compliance Officer Must Know

The Monetary Authority of Singapore revised its AML/CFT guidelines effective 1 July 2025 — introducing an explicit Proliferation Financing mandate and tightening STR timelines for sanctioned-party cases. Here is what changed, what it means for your programme, and the actions you need to take before the deadline.

Singapore's financial sector woke up to a significant regulatory shift on 1 July 2025. The MAS revised its suite of AML/CFT instruments — including the Guidelines on Prevention of Money Laundering and Countering the Financing of Terrorism for Direct General Insurance Business, Reinsurance Business, and Direct Life Insurance Business — introducing changes that every compliance officer in a MAS-licensed institution needs to understand and act on.

This article focuses specifically on what changed, why it matters for reinsurers and insurers in Singapore, and the concrete steps you should be taking now.

What Changed on 1 July 2025

The July 2025 revisions introduced three material changes across the MAS AML/CFT framework:

1. Proliferation Financing (PF) Now Explicitly Mandated

The most significant change is the formal incorporation of Proliferation Financing (PF) risk assessments into the AML/CFT framework. Previously, PF was addressed indirectly through sanctions compliance obligations. From 1 July 2025, institutions are explicitly required to:

  • Conduct a formal PF risk assessment as part of their institutional risk assessment process
  • Identify PF-specific red flags — particularly relating to DPRK and Iran nexus transactions
  • Include PF in staff training programmes
  • Document PF controls separately from general sanctions controls

Why this matters for reinsurers: Reinsurance transactions often involve cross-border premium flows through jurisdictions that are subject to DPRK and Iran sanctions. Marine, aviation, and trade credit reinsurance are particularly exposed. A formal PF risk assessment forces reinsurers to explicitly map their exposure — which is a more demanding standard than the previous implicit approach.

2. Tightened STR Timeline for Sanctioned-Party Cases

MAS Notice 626 (revised) now imposes a 1 business day Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) filing deadline where the transaction involves a party on a sanctions list. Previously, the standard STR filing timeline applied (within a practicable period, generally interpreted as a few business days).

This creates a significant operational requirement: your STR process must be able to identify sanctioned-party matches and generate a compliant STR filing within a single business day. For most compliance teams, this will require a review of the operational workflow between sanctions screening and STR filing.

3. Clarification of MAS Notice 314 Scope

The revisions also clarify that MAS Notice 314 applies only to direct life insurers writing life policies. Reinsurers are governed by the separate MAS AML/CFT Guidelines for Reinsurance Business. This distinction is important — compliance programmes built on Notice 314 requirements may need to be reviewed and updated to reference the correct instrument.

Regulatory Reference

MAS Notice 314 — Prevention of ML/TF for Direct Life Insurers (life policies only). Cancelled and re-issued 1 July 2025.

MAS AML/CFT Guidelines (Reinsurance Business) — Separate instrument governing reinsurers. Updated 1 July 2025 to include PF mandate and 1 business day STR requirement for sanctioned-party cases.

Your Action Checklist

What You Need to Do Before Year-End 2025

  • Conduct a formal PF risk assessment — document your institution's exposure to DPRK and Iran-linked transactions by line of business
  • Update your AML/CFT policy to include a PF section — reference the correct MAS instrument (AML/CFT Guidelines for Reinsurance Business, not Notice 314)
  • Review your STR filing workflow — can you generate a compliant STR within 1 business day of a sanctioned-party match?
  • Update staff training to include PF red flags and the new 1-business-day STR requirement
  • Review your sanctions screening schedule — periodic re-screening is required, not just onboarding screening

The Broader Context: FATF and MAS Alignment

The July 2025 changes reflect MAS's ongoing alignment with the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) framework, particularly FATF Recommendation 7 on targeted financial sanctions relating to proliferation financing. Following FATF's 2020 guidance on PF risk assessment and mitigation, most major financial regulators globally have been systematically incorporating explicit PF obligations into their AML/CFT frameworks.

Singapore's update is consistent with what the FCA implemented in the UK (via revised MLRs guidance), the EBA's December 2025 guidelines, and FinCEN's updated expectations. Compliance officers managing multi-jurisdictional programmes should treat the July 2025 MAS changes as consistent with the global direction of travel — not as a Singapore-specific outlier.

The Marine Reinsurance Sanctions Blind Spot: Five Things Your Compliance Programme Is Probably Missing

Marine reinsurance carries the highest sanctions exposure of any line of business — yet most compliance programmes treat it the same as property. Here are the five gaps that consistently leave reinsurers exposed during regulatory examinations.

In over a decade of compliance practice across reinsurance markets, one pattern repeats itself with striking consistency: marine reinsurance compliance is systematically under-resourced relative to the sanctions risk it carries. Compliance programmes that are robust for property catastrophe, casualty, and even life reinsurance often have significant gaps when it comes to the specific obligations that attach to marine and energy risks.

The reason is structural. Marine risks are complex, international, and involve a web of counterparties — vessel owners, operators, charterers, cargo interests, brokers — many of whom are several layers removed from the cedant relationship. Each of these layers represents a potential sanctions exposure point. Yet many compliance frameworks focus only on the direct cedant relationship, missing the downstream exposure entirely.

Here are the five gaps most commonly identified during regulatory examinations of reinsurance compliance programmes with marine book exposure.

Gap 1 — No Vessel-Level Screening Protocol

The most fundamental gap is the absence of a vessel-level screening process. Many reinsurers screen their cedants and brokers at onboarding — which is necessary but not sufficient for marine risks. A compliant cedant can submit a risk involving a vessel that is owned by a sanctioned entity, flagged in a high-risk jurisdiction, or has recently made port calls in sanctioned territories.

A proper vessel screening protocol covers:

  • IMO Number verification — the permanent vessel identifier, unchanged even if the vessel is renamed or re-flagged
  • Beneficial ownership tracing — through the multi-layer corporate structures commonly used in the shipping industry
  • AIS (Automatic Identification System) history — specifically, any dark vessel behavior in the previous 12 months
  • Port call history — any calls at sanctioned ports (Bandar Abbas, Latakia, etc.) in the previous 12 months
  • Flag state assessment — vessels using open registries known to be used by sanctioned actors

The OFAC 50% Rule applies to vessel ownership. Under OFAC rules, any entity that is 50% or more owned by a Specially Designated National is itself blocked — even if not explicitly named on the SDN list. In marine reinsurance, this requires tracing beneficial ownership through what are often complex multi-jurisdictional corporate structures. This cannot be satisfied by a simple name-match screen.

Gap 2 — No Cargo Classification Review

Not all cargo is equal from a sanctions compliance perspective. Cargo screening is a distinct obligation from vessel screening, and many marine compliance programmes conflate the two or omit cargo screening entirely.

Cargo CategoryCompliance Consideration
Crude oil & petroleum productsRussian, Iranian, Venezuelan origin — price cap rules, origin verification essential
Dual-use goodsTechnology, chemicals, electronics — may require export licence verification
Grain & agricultural commoditiesRussian-origin goods subject to EU/UK/US restrictions
Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG)Russian LNG subject to US, EU, UK energy financing restrictions

Gap 3 — No Pre-Claims Re-Screening Process

Vessel ownership changes. A vessel that was clean at inception may have been sold to a sanctioned entity by the time a claim arises. Yet most marine compliance programmes only screen at the point of placement — not at the point of claims payment.

Before authorising or recommending payment of any marine claim, the payee should be re-screened. This is not a theoretical risk: there have been well-documented cases in the London market where claims were submitted on vessels whose ownership had changed to SDN-linked entities following the inception of the policy.

Gap 4 — Missing Sanctions Clause Verification

The LMA Joint Sanctions Clause (LMA 3100) is the market standard for marine placements. Many compliance frameworks assume it is always present — without actually verifying its inclusion or reviewing its terms. The specific trigger conditions in the clause (what constitutes automatic termination, what requires active cancellation, how partial coverage situations are handled) are material compliance considerations that should be reviewed at the time of placement, not assumed.

Gap 5 — No Aggregation Monitoring for High-Risk Geographies

Marine catastrophe accumulation is well-managed by underwriting teams. Sanctions accumulation is frequently not. A reinsurance portfolio can build up significant exposure to vessels transiting the Red Sea, the Strait of Hormuz, or the Black Sea — all areas that carry elevated sanctions risk — without any compliance-level awareness of the aggregate exposure.

Five Immediate Actions for Marine Reinsurance Compliance

  • Implement a vessel screening checklist for all new marine and energy risks — covering IMO number, beneficial ownership, AIS history, port calls, and flag state
  • Add cargo type to your bordereaux data requirements — you cannot screen what you do not capture
  • Build a pre-claims re-screening step into your claims handling process for all marine risks above a defined threshold
  • Verify the presence and terms of LMA 3100 (or equivalent) in every new marine placement as a compliance sign-off item
  • Create a geographic concentration report for marine risks in sanctioned or high-risk zones — review quarterly

AML/CFT Guide March 2025 · 7 min read

From Policy to Practice: Why Most AML/CFT Programmes Fail the Examination — and How to Fix Yours

Regulatory examinations consistently find the same gap: well-written policies that are not operationalized. This is the practical framework that turns compliance policy documents into working programmes — applicable to both AML/CFT and reinsurance compliance.

After reviewing dozens of compliance programmes across financial institutions in Singapore, the UK, and Hong Kong, one observation stands out above all others: the gap between a well-written policy and a functioning compliance programme is wider than almost anyone admits.

Most compliance failures that result in regulatory enforcement action are not caused by the absence of a policy. They are caused by the failure to operationalize the policy — to translate abstract compliance requirements into specific controls, assign those controls to named owners, test whether the controls are working, and fix them when they are not.

This article sets out the framework we use — which we call the PACE Model — for turning any compliance policy into a working operational programme.

Why Policies Alone Are Not Enough

Consider a typical AML/CFT policy statement: "The institution will conduct sanctions screening on all customers at onboarding and on an ongoing basis."

This statement is perfectly adequate as a policy. But it tells you almost nothing about how compliance actually happens. Who conducts the screening? What system or list do they use? What happens when there is a match? What does "ongoing basis" mean in practice — monthly, quarterly, on a trigger? What evidence is kept? Who reviews the evidence? What happens when the process breaks down?

Regulators do not examine your policy document. They examine the evidence that your controls are working. The gap between the policy statement and the evidence of operational compliance is exactly where most programmes are found wanting.

The PACE Model

The PACE Model is a four-step framework for operationalizing any compliance policy. It is designed to be repeatable — applicable to a new sanctions policy, an updated AML/CFT guideline, or a reinsurance compliance framework. The steps are:

P — Parse the Policy

Break the policy down into its individual mandatory requirements. Every sentence containing "must", "shall", "required", or "prohibited" represents a separate compliance obligation that needs its own control. A policy that reads as a coherent paragraph in a board-approved document often contains six or eight distinct operational requirements when parsed carefully.

A — Assign Ownership and Design Controls

For each parsed requirement, assign a named owner (a role, not a team) and design a specific control. A control is a defined action, process, or system that reduces the likelihood or impact of a compliance failure. The four control types that matter are: preventive (stop the problem before it happens), detective (identify problems after they occur), corrective (fix identified problems), and directive (guide behaviour proactively).

C — Communicate and Train

A control that is not understood by the people responsible for it will not work. For each key compliance process, produce a one-page "What Do I Do If..." guide covering three scenarios: everything is normal, something looks unusual, and something has clearly gone wrong. Measure comprehension, not just completion.

E — Execute, Monitor and Improve

Run the controls, test them regularly, and fix gaps when testing reveals them. A finding from your own testing is infinitely preferable to a finding from a regulator.

The Four Questions That Define a Working Control

  • Is there a documented control for this requirement — not just a policy statement?
  • Is there a named owner for this control — not a team, a specific role?
  • Is there evidence that the control has been applied — retrievable, dated, signed?
  • Has the control been tested in the last 12 months — with findings documented and remediated?

The Most Common Operationalization Failures

Failure ModeWhat It Looks LikeHow to Fix It
Policy without control"We have a sanctions policy" but no screening processParse the policy — identify each requirement — design a specific control for each
Control without owner"Compliance team" owns the controlAssign a specific role — if everyone owns it, no one does

Applying This to Your AML/CFT Programme

If you are reviewing your AML/CFT programme ahead of a regulatory examination, the quickest diagnostic is to pick your three highest-risk controls — sanctions screening, STR filing, and KYC refresh, for example — and ask the four questions above for each one. If you cannot answer all four questions with documentary evidence, you have identified your examination risk before the regulator does.

The same framework applies directly to reinsurance compliance. The operationalization gap is just as common in treaty compliance, bordereaux monitoring, and LOB-specific controls as it is in AML/CFT.
